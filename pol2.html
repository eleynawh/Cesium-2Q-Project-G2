Man Down

Conservative activist Charlie Kirk was shot while on his "American Comeback" tour on September 10, 2025 at Utah Valley University. This event happened when Charlie Kirk showed up for the debate on Wednesday at the UVU campus courtyard area, which is located right outside the Hall of Flags. In there, a tent bearing his "PROVE ME WRONG" branding was present, guarded by two rows of safety barriers.

The American Comeback tour started. As he was answering someone's question about transgender mass shooters, a loud “pop” interrupted: it was a gunshot. When people heard the gunshot, the place turned into chaos, everybody was running, screaming, and yelling. Reporter Emma pits stated, "his neck kind of turned, and we saw blood immediately.

A few moments later, outside there was a group of bodyguards carrying Kirk out to an SUV then he was moved to a private vehicle and taken to Timpanogos Regional Hospital. After a while US President Donald Trump announced his death writing, "The Great, and even Legendary, Charlie Kirk, is dead.”

On September 12, 2025, Tyler Robinson, the man accused of shooting right-wing activist Charlie Kirk, was arrested and charged with aggravated murder. According to Robinson's mother, Robinson was becoming more political over the years and had started to lean more to the left becoming more pro-gay and trans-rights.

Authorities released messages between Robinson and his unnamed roommate which they said was a man transitioning to woman and was in a romantic relationship with the defendant. In the message, the roommate asked, "Why?," in which Robinson responded with "I had enough of his hatred," "some hate can't be negotiated out." Also, according to Attorney Jeff Gray, Robinson also left a note for his roommate, which allegedly said "I had the opportunity to take out Charlie Kirk, and I'm going to take it."

Katherine Schweit, a former FBI agent and prosecutor, said investigators will be busy tracking Robinson's online records to get more information and "piece them all together to see how someone moves on the pathway from frustration all the way to choosing to commit a violent act." She also said, "TV and movies have taught us that the motive is the most important thing, but from a prosecution standpoint, it's not." She noted that some assassinations were only motivated by the desire to be famous or impress people.
